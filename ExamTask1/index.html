White Rhino

The white rhinoceros is the largest of all of current rhino species. The origin of the name, white whino, is unknown. A name better suited to it is its alternative name, the square-lipped rhino. The square lips are perfectly adapted for it to eat its diet of grass. With it can forage grass efficiently and does not have pick grass pieces one by one. It has two horns, of which the one further away from the face is larger.

Subspecies

There are two sub species of the white rhino : the southern and the northern. There are approximately 20000 southern white rhinos in the wild. Its conservation status is Not Threatened. They can be found in southern Africa, in nations like South Africa, Namibia, etc. It has also been introduced to east Africa, in Kenya and Tanzania.

The northern white rhino has only three remaining individuals left. It is essentially functionally extinct, meaning that there is no realistic chance that the population can recover. It used to be able to found in DRC Congo and Uganda, but now all individuals came from a zoo in Czech Republic and live in Kenya.

Did you know?

  • According to the ranger who was guided me through Kruger National Park, white rhinos are the only rhinos which can not swim. This is due to them not being able to lift their high enough, as they forage only on the ground.

Sumatran Rhino

The Sumatran rhino is most threatened of all rhinos. There are about 100 left, but they are fragmented the population is decreasing. The Sumatran looks the most distinct of all rhinos with its layer of thin air on its body. There are three subspecies : Eastern, Western and Chittagong. The Chittagong subspecies is extinct and used to be found in India and Bangaladesh. The Eastern Sumatran rhino has about 15 individuals in Borneo. The Western subspecies lives in Sumatra, but is extinct in West Malaysia. The individuals live in 4 fragmented populations. There about 10 living in captivity.

Did you know?

  • In the 1980s 40 rhinos were transported to zoos around the world. They adapted horrendously to captivity and only three remained by 1997. Fortunately, in 2001 the first captive birth of a Sumatran Rhino was observed, with three more in the years following.

Javan Rhino

The Javan rhino is the rarest of all rhino species. With only about 60 rhinos left and none in captivity, the Javan rhino is critically endangered. There used to be three subspecies: Indonesian, Vietnamese and Indian. The Indian and Vietnamese subspecies are both extinct. They only have one horn and look quite similar to Indian rhinos, but smaller in size.

Did you know?

  • There was and is still to this day very little known about Javan rhinos. The last Javan rhino is captivity was displayed as Indian rhino over a century ago.
  • The Vietnamese population was presumed to have become extinct due to the Vietname War, but they somehow survived and were discovered again in 1988. Unfortunately the last rhino in Vietnam died in 2010.

Rhino poaching

Rhino poaching is an issue which is threatening the continued existence of rhinos in the wild. Before the 2000s, rhinos were hunted for their horns, which were used as decorations in the Middle East, making the population collapse. Fortunately this stopped by the year 2000. As seen in the chart below, for the last few years, more than a 1000 rhinos die every year. Rhinos are poached for their horn, which are used in traditional chinese medicine. Rhino horn is made of keratin, which is the same material as human finger/toe nails. This means that rhino horn also has the same medicinal properties. Since finger nails are actually not used in medicine, this of course means that rhino horn also have no medicinal benefits.

Rhinos are slaughtered for medicine based on superstition. While in the 2000s poaching rates were low, the increased wealth of China and Vietnam have increased the interest in traditional chinese medicine. Prices for rhino horn reportedly are higher than prices for gold and platinum. This allowed poachers to use sophisticated equipment like machine guns and helicopters, while rangers use normal rifles. Unfortunately, rhinos are not the only species threatened by traditional chinese medicine. Elephants, tigers, pangolins, vaquitas and many other species are at risk.
